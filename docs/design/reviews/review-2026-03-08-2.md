## Synthesis: R5 Knowledge Quality — 7-Model Review

Seven models reviewed the R5 "Knowledge Quality" design across vision, requirements, and spec. The consensus is that the rewrite direction is strong — agent-driven feedback replacing human rating ceremonies, low-friction signal collection, and suggested-not-automatic retirement are all praised unanimously. The problems are concentrated in under-specification and internal inconsistencies, not over-engineering.

### High-Confidence Findings (Multiple Reviewers Agree)

**Utility score aggregation is unspecified.** All seven reviewers flagged this. The spec says "rolling average or similar" for computing `utility_score` from individual `artifact_feedback` records, but "or similar" leaves cold start behavior, recency weighting, anti-spam policy, and sample-size sensitivity undefined. Since both retrieval ranking and retirement candidacy depend on this value, it is a core design decision masquerading as an implementation detail. Severity ranges from Medium (Claude, Kimi, GLM-5, MiniMax) to High (GPT-5.4, GPT-5.3), but no reviewer considered it negligible.

**The `review_grades` table is too thin for its stated purpose.** Six of seven reviewers (all except MiniMax, who noted it partially) identified that the table lacks a `review_type` column despite the requirements explicitly calling for "per-model, per-review-type" queryability and the `get_review_quality` helper accepting a `review_type?` parameter. The spec claims this helper needs "no joins," which is false without the column. Five reviewers (Claude, Kimi, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.3, GLM-5) also noted that the grading prompt asks for a four-level scale (SUCCEEDED/PARTIAL_SUCCESS/PARTIAL_FAILURE/FAILED) that collapses to `useful BOOLEAN` in storage, discarding signal the system explicitly asked for. Medium-to-High severity across reviewers.

**Vision and requirements have semantic drift.** Five reviewers (Claude, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.3, GLM-5, MiniMax) identified that the vision promises outcome-weighted learning ("what approaches worked for problems like this?") while R5 delivers retrieval-usefulness feedback ("was this artifact helpful when retrieved?"). These are related but not equivalent signals. Claude rated this Low (conscious scope reduction), while GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.3 rated it High (the existing codebase still embodies the old outcome model in `artifact_store.py`, `artifact_models.py`, and `migrations/001_initial_schema.sql`, creating implementation confusion). The gap is real; the severity depends on whether you view the vision as aspirational or prescriptive.

**`last_retrieved` is a phantom column.** Three reviewers (Claude, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.3) caught that `artifact_retirement_candidates` returns `last_retrieved` in its response, but no such column exists in the schema and no retrieval telemetry is recorded. `artifact_feedback` only captures retrievals where feedback was explicitly given, not all retrievals. Claude rated this High; the others Medium. The retirement flow depends on this field.

**Spec changelog contradicts the schema.** Three reviewers (Gemini, GPT-5.4, GPT-5.3) noted that the spec's "Changes from Draft 8" says "R5.5 now uses `artifact_feedback`" while the actual schema uses a separate `review_grades` table. The separation is the correct architecture (all reviewers agree on this), but the stale changelog language will confuse implementers. Medium severity.

**No recency signal in scoring.** Three reviewers (Claude, Kimi, GLM-5) noted the vision explicitly mentions recency affecting ranking, but the scoring formula has no age component. An artifact from 2024 with positive historical feedback ranks identically to an equivalent 2026 artifact. Medium severity; could be deferred as a future enhancement but represents a stated vision gap.

**SUPERSEDED conflated with archived.** Three reviewers (Claude, GLM-5, Kimi indirectly) noted the search query ties SUPERSEDED visibility to the `include_archived` flag, conflating two semantically distinct concepts. Low-to-Minor severity — pragmatically harmless but imprecise.

**Retirement flow is appropriate.** All seven reviewers praise the suggested-not-automatic design as correct for knowledge management where automatic deletion risks destroying valuable signal.

The requirements acknowledge this implicitly by renaming from "Outcome Tracking" to "Knowledge Quality," but the vision still contains the stronger claim about outcome-weighted precedent. **Severity: Low.** The narrowing is reasonable and well-motivated; the residual vision language is aspirational, not prescriptive.

### 2. Feedback Signal Design

**R5.1 (Usage feedback):** Well-designed. The boolean useful/not-useful signal with optional note is appropriately lightweight. The spec correctly identifies that "low friction is critical; this should feel like a bookmark, not a review" (spec line 876). The `agent_id` field enables multi-agent signal accumulation.

However, there's an under-specification in the utility score aggregation. The spec says "Update the artifact's aggregate utility score (rolling average or similar)" (spec line 874). "Or similar" is doing heavy lifting. The scoring formula downstream (line 694) treats `utility_score` as a float in [0, 1] centered at 0.5, but the aggregation method is unspecified. This matters because:

- A simple rolling average is dominated by early feedback (3 early "not useful" votes are hard to overcome even if the artifact becomes useful later).
- An exponentially-weighted moving average would favor recent signal but needs a decay parameter.
- A simple count ratio (useful / total) has no recency weighting at all.

The formula `(utility_score - 0.5) * 0.4` produces a range of [-0.2, +0.2], which is reasonable as a boost magnitude. But the *input* to that formula — how `utility_score` is computed from individual feedback records — is the part that needs specification.

**Severity: Medium.** The downstream formula is sound, but the aggregation method will significantly affect behavior and should be specified, not left to implementation.

**R5.2 (Confidence annotation):** Clean design. The distinction between confidence (a property of the content at write time, point-in-time, overwritten) and utility (accumulated from many retrievals) is well-drawn. SUPERSEDED as semantically distinct from LOW is a nice touch. The spec correctly notes this is "set at write time or updated when an agent discovers the information is stale" (spec line 897).

One subtlety: who sets confidence? The requirements say "agents can annotate their confidence level" (req line 292), and the spec says it's set "at write time or updated when an agent discovers the information is stale." But there's no mechanism described for agents to *discover* staleness during normal work. An agent would need to: (a) retrieve an artifact, (b) notice it's wrong based on current context, and (c) proactively call `artifact_set_confidence` with SUPERSEDED. This is a sophisticated behavior that depends on agents being prompted or trained to do it. The requirements treat it as a natural part of work, but it's actually a new behavior that agents need to be explicitly instructed to perform.

**Severity: Low.** The tool exists, the semantics are clear, and getting agents to use it is a prompt engineering / workflow problem, not a design problem. But it's worth noting that confidence annotation may accumulate much more slowly than usage feedback in practice.

**R5.5 (Review quality grading):** The design of having the synthesis model grade reviewers is elegant — it leverages a natural grading moment where the model has already evaluated each review's contributions. The spec's justification for structured output here (line 1496: "the downstream consumer is code, not another model") is well-reasoned.

However, there's a type mismatch between the grading prompt and the storage schema. The grading prompt asks for a four-level rating: SUCCEEDED, PARTIAL_SUCCESS, PARTIAL_FAILURE, FAILED (spec line 1492). But the `review_grades` table stores a boolean `useful` column (spec line 227). The four-level scale collapses into a binary. Where does the threshold fall? Is PARTIAL_SUCCESS useful or not? The `note` column captures reasoning, but the queryable signal (`useful`) loses the granularity that the grading prompt produces.

**Severity: Medium.** Either the schema should store the four-level grade as a text column (matching the prompt), or the spec should document which grades map to `useful = true` vs `useful = false`. Right now the spec says one thing about what the synthesis model produces and another about what gets stored. The `get_review_quality` helper queries `review_grades` by model name — if it returns only booleans, the richer signal from the four-level grading is lost except in the `note` text.

### 3. Quality-Weighted Search Scoring Formula

The formula (spec lines 684-708) is sound in structure:

```
final_score = base_score + confidence_boost + utility_boost
```

Where:
- `base_score` = cosine similarity (0 to 1, typically 0.3-0.8 for meaningful matches)
- `confidence_boost` = [-0.2, +0.1] based on confidence level
- `utility_boost` = [-0.2, +0.2] based on utility score

The ranges are conservative — quality signal adjusts ranking within a semantic neighborhood but doesn't dominate it. A highly relevant low-confidence artifact still outranks a weakly relevant high-confidence one if the semantic gap is large enough. This is the right default behavior.

Two observations:

**SUPERSEDED exclusion logic is inconsistent.** Line 702: `AND (a.confidence != 'SUPERSEDED' OR include_archived)`. This ties SUPERSEDED visibility to the `include_archived` flag. But SUPERSEDED and archived are semantically different — SUPERSEDED means "replaced by newer information" while archived means "retired due to low utility." An artifact could be SUPERSEDED but not archived (still in the store, just outdated). The current logic means SUPERSEDED artifacts are invisible in default searches unless you also ask for archived content, which seems right in spirit but conflates two different flags. A user who wants archived content may not want superseded content, and vice versa. The requirements don't address this directly.

**Severity: Low.** In practice, SUPERSEDED items should almost always be excluded from default search, and users wanting them can use `include_archived`. The conflation is pragmatic, not harmful. But a dedicated `include_superseded` flag would be more precise.

**No recency signal.** The scoring formula has no recency component. An artifact from 2024 with high utility ranks the same as an identical one from 2026. The vision explicitly calls out that "recency and explicit supersession affect ranking" (vision line 185), but the spec only implements supersession, not recency. This is a gap between the vision's guidance and the spec's implementation.

**Severity: Medium.** For a growing knowledge base, recency is a useful ranking signal independent of confidence and utility. A simple `recency_boost` based on age (e.g., log-scaled days since creation) would close this gap without much complexity. However, this could be argued as a future enhancement rather than a Phase 4 requirement — the requirements don't explicitly call for recency weighting in R5.3.

Either the schema needs a `last_retrieved` timestamp on the artifacts table (updated on every retrieval), or `artifact_retirement_candidates` should use `MAX(af.created_at)` from `artifact_feedback` as a proxy — but that only captures retrievals where feedback was given, not all retrievals.

**Severity: High.** This is a gap in the schema — a column referenced in tool output but never defined or populated. The retirement flow depends on it.

### 5. Retirement Flow

The retirement design (suggested, not automatic) is appropriate and well-aligned with the vision's principle that "the human decides what matters." The `artifact_retirement_candidates` tool provides a query interface that lists candidates based on multiple signals (low utility, low confidence, SUPERSEDED, age), and actual archiving requires a separate `artifact_archive` call. This is the right separation of concerns.

The retirement criteria (spec line 916: "low utility scores, low confidence, or SUPERSEDED status that are older than `min_age_days`") are sensible defaults. The configurable thresholds (`min_age_days` default 30, `max_utility` default 0.3) give reasonable starting points.

One consideration: retirement candidates are identified by combining signals (utility + confidence + age), but the relative weighting isn't specified. Is an artifact with utility 0.25 and HIGH confidence a candidate? What about utility 0.5 and SUPERSEDED confidence? The spec says "low utility scores, low confidence, or SUPERSEDED status" — note the "or" — which means any single bad signal plus sufficient age triggers candidacy. This is aggressive; an artifact that's genuinely useful (utility 0.8) but marked SUPERSEDED would appear as a candidate. The `or` should probably be more nuanced, or the query should let the caller filter.

**Severity: Low.** The tool returns candidates for human review, not for automatic action. Even if the candidate list is over-inclusive, the confirmation step catches false positives.

### 6. `review_grades` Table Design

The table is simple and fit for purpose: `job_id`, `model_name`, `useful`, `note`, `created_at`. It enables the core query: "how has model X performed across reviews?"

Issues beyond the type mismatch noted above (four-level grade → boolean):

- **No FK to reviews table.** The `job_id` links to a review run but there's no FK constraint. This is probably intentional (loose coupling), but it means orphaned grades can't be detected via constraint.
- **No `review_type` column.** The requirements' acceptance criterion (req line 309) says "Historical grades are queryable by model and review type." The `get_review_quality` helper signature includes `review_type?` (spec line 1704), but the `review_grades` table has no `review_type` column. To query by review type, you'd need to join through `job_id` → `reviews` → `artifact_id` → `artifacts` → `artifact_type`. This is feasible but contradicts the spec's own claim that `get_review_quality` is "a direct query against this table — no joins needed" (spec line 1494).

**Severity: Medium.** The missing `review_type` column means either the table needs an additional column, or the "no joins needed" claim is wrong and the helper needs a join. Either fix is straightforward, but the current spec is inconsistent with itself.

### 7. Over-Engineering vs. Under-Specification

The overall design is well-calibrated — it solves the stated problems without excessive machinery. The feedback signals are appropriately lightweight, the scoring formula is conservative, and the retirement flow has proper guardrails.

**Under-specified areas:**
- Utility score aggregation method (noted above)
- `last_retrieved` tracking mechanism
- Review grade → boolean mapping
- `review_type` in grades

If the system only stores and queries a boolean, the four levels add prompt complexity for no stored benefit. Either store the four-level grade or simplify the grading prompt to binary.

### Design of Feedback Signals

### Quality-Weighted Search Scoring Formula

The search scoring formula presented in the `spec` (Section 4.1, `artifact_search`) is sound and well-reasoned.

`final_score = base_score + confidence_boost + utility_boost`

-   The `base_score` is standard vector cosine similarity.
-   The `confidence_boost` provides a simple, effective heuristic. The chosen values (-0.2 to +0.1) are modest enough that they won't completely override semantic relevance but are significant enough to make a difference.
-   The `utility_boost` (`(a.utility_score - 0.5) * 0.4`) is also well-designed. It correctly centers the boost around a neutral score of 0.5, rewarding artifacts that are consistently useful and penalizing those that are not, without creating a bias against new, un-rated artifacts.

This formula directly implements the goals of R5.3 and is a good balance between semantic relevance and learned quality. **Severity: Low (Praise).**

### Retirement Flow

The retirement flow (R5.4) is appropriate and well-considered. It avoids the pitfall of automatic deletion, which could lead to unintended data loss.

-   The flow is **suggestion-based**, not automatic. The `artifact_retirement_candidates` tool identifies potential candidates based on clear, tunable criteria (age, utility, confidence).

The formula (Section 4.1, lines 682-709) has mathematical issues:

```sql
COALESCE(utility_boost, 0) AS final_score
```

**Problems:**

1. **Range instability**: Cosine distance returns -1 to 1, so `1 - distance` is 0 to 2. Adding ±0.3 in boosts gives a final range of roughly -0.3 to 2.3. This is unbounded and non-comparable across queries.

3. **No normalization**: As the corpus grows, raw similarity scores drift. Without normalization, ranking becomes unstable.

**Recommendation:** Normalize the base score to 0-1, then apply weighted boosts with explicit coefficients that favor utility over confidence (e.g., utility_boost × 0.6, confidence_boost × 0.4).

# Review: R5 Knowledge Quality

## 1. Overall Coherence

The three documents tell a coherent story about knowledge quality. The vision introduced "outcome-weighted feedback" as part of "Persistent Context That Compounds" — the idea that what worked should rank higher in future retrieval. The requirements renamed this "Knowledge Quality" (R5.1-R5.5), reorienting from human-initiated rating ceremonies to agent-driven usage feedback. The spec implements this with:
- `artifact_feedback` table capturing per-retrieval signal
- `confidence` and `utility_score` columns on artifacts
- Quality-weighted search scoring
- `review_grades` table for per-model signal

This is a clean evolution. One minor drift: the vision says "outcome-weighted feedback" while requirements/spec use "usage feedback" + "utility score" — same concept, different terminology. Not a contradiction, but could confuse someone reading all three.

## 2. Feedback Signal Design

**R5.1 (usage feedback):** Well-designed. The constraint that "signal must emerge from work that's already happening" is smart — agents retrieving knowledge are already in context, so recording usefulness is low-friction. The spec captures `useful` (boolean), `note`, and `agent_id`. This is appropriately lightweight.

One gap: there's no explicit mechanism for the retrieval flow to trigger feedback awareness. The spec doesn't define how an agent knows to call `artifact_feedback` after using retrieved content. The requirements say "at the point of use" but don't specify how the system makes this flow natural. This could lead to sparse feedback in practice.

**R5.2 (confidence annotation):** Good design. The separation of confidence (write-time assessment, point-in-time) from usage feedback (accumulated signal from multiple retrievals) is sound. The four levels (HIGH, MEDIUM, LOW, SUPERSEDED) are meaningful — SUPERSEDED is particularly well-designed as "not wrong, just outdated."

The spec's `artifact_set_confidence` tool accepts a `reason` parameter, but this isn't stored anywhere — it's lost after the call. This seems like a missed opportunity. The reason could be valuable for auditing why confidence changed.

**R5.5 (review quality grading):** Appropriately leverages the synthesis model. The spec notes this is "an exception to the 'no structured output' principle because the downstream consumer is code (database inserts), not another model" — a reasonable exception.

The `review_grades` table design is clean:
```sql
CREATE TABLE review_grades (
    id UUID PRIMARY KEY,
    job_id UUID NOT NULL,
    model_name TEXT NOT NULL,
    useful BOOLEAN NOT NULL,
    note TEXT,
    created_at TIMESTAMPTZ
);
```

However, the spec mentions "historical grades are queryable by model and review type" but `review_type` isn't in the schema. This is a gap — the table captures model and usefulness but not what kind of review this was (code review? design doc review? security review?). For accumulating useful per-model signal, this matters.

## 3. Quality-Weighted Search Scoring

The formula at spec lines 684-709 is:

```sql
1 - (ae.embedding <=> $1::vector) AS base_score,  -- cosine distance
+ confidence boost: HIGH +0.1, MEDIUM 0, LOW -0.1, SUPERSEDED -0.2
+ utility boost: (utility_score - 0.5) * 0.4  -- range -0.2 to +0.2
```

This is sound. A few observations:

1. **No-signal artifacts get neutral treatment** — correct per requirements. New artifacts start with utility_score 0.5, so they get no boost or penalty.

2. **SUPERSEDED explicitly excluded from default search** — the WHERE clause `AND (a.confidence != 'SUPERSEDED' OR include_archived)` ensures superseded content doesn't pollute results. This is good design.

3. **The boost magnitudes seem reasonable.** Confidence ±0.1 and utility ±0.2 on a base score of 0-1 gives meaningful differentiation without dominating semantic similarity. A highly relevant but low-confidence artifact could still rank below a moderately relevant high-confidence one.

One concern: there's no definition of how `utility_score` is computed from `artifact_feedback` entries. The spec says "Update the artifact's aggregate utility score (rolling average or similar)" — "or similar" is too vague. Different aggregation strategies would produce very different behavior:
- Simple average of all feedback
- Decay-weighted recent feedback
- Threshold-based (e.g., 3+ positive = boost)

This needs specification.

## 4. Gaps and Contradictions

**Contradictions:** None significant. The documents align well.

**Gaps:**

1. **Utility score aggregation is undefined** — spec says "rolling average or similar" which is under-specified for a core scoring component.

2. **Review type not in review_grades** — mentioned above.

3. **Confidence reason not stored** — `artifact_set_confidence` accepts `reason` but it's not persisted.

4. **Feedback sparsity concern** — no mechanism ensures agents actually provide feedback. The flow is: retrieve → use → call feedback tool. But nothing guides the agent to do this. The requirements acknowledge this is a design assumption ("agents can emit during normal work") but don't ensure it happens.

5. **Retirement criteria underspecified** — R5.4 says "consistently low utility" but the spec's `artifact_retirement_candidates` tool uses `max_utility` threshold (default 0.3). "Consistently" isn't modeled — a single low score triggers retirement candidates the same as multiple low scores.

## 5. Retirement Flow Appropriateness

"Suggested, not automatic" is correct. Auto-archival of knowledge is high-risk — unlike code where you can always git revert, retired knowledge is harder to recover. The spec's design (flag candidates, require explicit `artifact_archive` call) is appropriate.

The tool parameters (min_age_days, max_utility) provide reasonable controls. One missing dimension: confidence SUPERSEDED should probably auto-include as retirement candidates regardless of utility score, since it's explicitly "outdated" information. Currently it doesn't — the query would need to explicitly include SUPERSEDED.

## 6. review_grades Table Design

The table properly captures per-model signal. One improvement opportunity: add `review_type` as mentioned in the spec's text ("queryable by model and review type"). Without it, you can't distinguish "this model is good at code reviews" from "this model is good at design doc reviews."

The job_id foreign key is correct — it links grades back to the specific review run, enabling auditability ("why did we rate this model poorly?").

## 7. Over-Engineering and Under-Specification

**Over-engineering:** Not much. The three-signal design (usage feedback, confidence, review grades) is appropriately layered. The decision to make review grading use structured output (exception to model-forward principle) is justified.

**Under-specification:**
1. Utility score aggregation strategy (critical gap)
2. Feedback triggering mechanism — how do agents know to provide feedback?
3. Review type dimension in grades
4. Confidence reason storage
5. "Consistently" in retirement criteria — not modeled

## Summary

The R5 design is fundamentally sound. The agent-driven feedback model aligns with the "signal emerges from work already happening" principle. The separation of confidence (write-time) from usage feedback (accumulated retrieval signal) is clean. Quality-weighted search scoring uses appropriate boost magnitudes.

The main gaps are around aggregation mechanics (how feedback becomes utility_score) and ensuring feedback actually happens in practice. These are implementation-details-that-matter rather than design flaws — the spec correctly identifies them as things to resolve during Phase 4 implementation.
