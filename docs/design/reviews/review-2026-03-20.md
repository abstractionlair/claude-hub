## 1. Existing Structured Representations of Research

### Papers With Code (PwC)

**What it actually provides.** PwC maintains a crowdsourced taxonomy of ~1,064 methods organized into six top-level areas (Computer Vision: 665, NLP: 119, Graphs: 104, RL: 88, Sequential: 53, Audio: 35). Methods have parent-child relationships — "Attention" contains "Multi-Head Attention," "Scaled Dot-Product Attention," etc. Methods link to papers, and papers link to benchmarks via a Task → Dataset → Metric → Score leaderboard structure. A REST API exposes Paper, Method, Task, Dataset, and Repository models. The full dataset is downloadable as JSON.

**What's useful for this project.** PwC's method-to-benchmark linkage is directly relevant to Stage 2 (eval matrix construction) and Stage 3 (progress decomposition). The method taxonomy is a rough first-pass at the kind of vocabulary the vision describes. The leaderboard data — model × benchmark × score — is the closest existing public dataset to the eval matrix the vision needs.

**What's missing / known limitations.** A 2025 study (CEUR-WS Vol-3977) found PwC's categories have ambiguous boundaries — methods span multiple fields, making strict classification unreliable. The taxonomy is shallow (parent-child only, no composition semantics), static (no version history of how the taxonomy evolved), and coarse (it names methods but doesn't parameterize them). PwC knows "DPO" exists and links it to papers, but doesn't represent DPO's constructor signature — its parameters, what it replaces, what it composes with. There's no formal relationship vocabulary beyond parent-child. The vision's AST-of-constructor-calls representation is a fundamentally different granularity than PwC's flat method registry. **However**: PwC's leaderboard data is a steal-worthy asset. The eval matrix (Stage 2) should ingest PwC scores rather than rebuilding from scratch.

### Open Research Knowledge Graph (ORKG)

**What it actually provides.** ORKG (orkg.org) is an RDF/OWL-based platform where papers are described as structured contributions with typed properties. Templates (based on SHACL) specify expected schemas — e.g., leaderboards follow a Task–Dataset–Metric–Value pattern. The core feature is *comparisons*: tabular views where multiple papers' contributions are aligned property-by-property. Comparisons can be filtered and visualized. The system supports both manual curation and NLP-assisted extraction.

**What's useful.** ORKG's comparison tables are conceptually close to what the vision calls "the type system at HEAD" — a structured, property-aligned view of how different papers' innovations relate. Their template system is relevant to the extraction problem: templates constrain what the LLM needs to produce, reducing the consistency challenge the vision correctly identifies as the critical risk. ORKG also bridges to SciKGTeX, a LaTeX package that lets authors annotate contributions in-paper (7 minutes per abstract, 79/100 usability score), which could eventually be an upstream data source.

**What's missing.** ORKG is general-purpose — it structures *any* research, not AI methods specifically. Its templates describe properties of contributions but don't represent compositional relationships between methods. There's no concept of "FlashAttention composes with Transformer" or "DPO replaces RLHF." Its schema is relational (property-value pairs), not algebraic (ASTs of constructors). The vision's core innovation — treating method specifications as composable expressions with formal parameter spaces — is genuinely beyond what ORKG captures. ORKG also suffers from the cold-start problem: most papers aren't in it, and curation is manual and slow.

### Semantic Scholar / S2ORC

**What it actually provides.** The Semantic Scholar Academic Graph (S2AG) contains hundreds of millions of papers with citation links, author disambiguation, venue classification, and semantic features (embeddings, TLDR summaries). S2ORC provides full-text annotations: section headings, paragraphs, bibliography entries, inline citations, table/figure references. The API is free, well-documented, and supports bulk access.

**What's useful.** S2's citation graph and paper embeddings are foundational infrastructure for any project working with research papers. The TLDR summaries and section-level annotations could feed Stage 1 extraction. Citation data enables the kind of lineage tracking the vision needs. Critically, S2's embeddings provide a continuous representation of paper similarity that could complement the discrete type system — useful for detecting when two papers are "about the same thing" even if their formal fragments differ.

**What's missing.** S2 doesn't structure *methods*. It knows papers exist and cite each other, but doesn't know what FlashAttention *is* or how DPO relates to RLHF beyond citation links. It's bibliographic infrastructure, not semantic infrastructure. The vision needs to build the method-level semantics on top of what S2 provides.

### OpenAlex

**What it actually provides.** The successor to Microsoft Academic Graph, OpenAlex indexes 209M works, 2B disambiguated authors, 124K venues, 109K institutions, and 65K Wikidata concepts linked to works via automated hierarchical multi-tag classification. Fully open, with a REST API handling 115M monthly queries (2024). Funded as critical open science infrastructure ($7.5M Arcadia grant).

**What's useful.** OpenAlex's concept tagging is a machine-generated analog to the vision's type system, but at the topic level rather than the method level. Its scale and openness make it the best source for paper metadata. The hierarchical concept classifier is an existence proof that automated topic classification works at scale — which is encouraging for the extraction challenge.

**What's missing.** Same gap as S2: concepts are topics ("reinforcement learning"), not methods ("DPO with sigmoid Bradley-Terry loss"). No compositional representation, no parameter spaces, no versioning. OpenAlex tells you a paper is *about* attention mechanisms; it doesn't tell you *which* attention mechanism with *what* configuration.

### Computer Science Ontology (CSO)

**What it actually provides.** CSO is an automatically generated ontology of ~14K topics and 162K semantic relationships, built by applying the Klink-2 algorithm to 16M scientific articles. It's far more granular than ACM's classification (~2K topics, last updated 2012). CSO powers tools at Springer Nature for classifying publications, detecting research communities, and predicting trends. It can be updated automatically by re-running Klink-2 on new corpora.

**What's useful.** CSO demonstrates that large-scale automatic ontology generation from paper corpora *works* — a direct precedent for the vision's extraction pipeline. The Klink-2 algorithm (statistical co-occurrence of terms in papers, processed through a series of semantic similarity steps) is a simpler, pre-LLM approach to a similar goal. CSO's experience with ontology *evolution* (re-running on new data) is relevant to the versioning question.

**What's missing.** CSO captures *topics* (research areas), not *methods* (specific techniques with parameters). "Attention mechanisms" is a CSO topic; "FlashAttention(version=2, block_size=128)" is not. CSO's relationships are taxonomic (broader/narrower) and associative (related), not compositional. This is exactly the gap the vision aims to fill.

## 2. Science of Science / Metascience Literature

### The Disruption Index (CD Index)

**What it is.** Funk & Owen-Smith (2017) introduced the CD index for patents; Wu, Wang & Evans (2019) applied it to bibliometrics. The index measures whether a paper is "disruptive" (future papers cite it but NOT its references, suggesting it displaced prior work) or "consolidating" (future papers cite both it and its references, suggesting it built incrementally). Park, Leahey & Funk (2023) used this to argue that science is becoming less disruptive over time.

**Relevance to this project.** The CD index operationalizes something close to what the vision calls "REMOVES_DEPENDENCY_ON" vs. "composes with." A highly disruptive paper in CD terms is one that breaks the citation chain — analogous to introducing a new class that replaces rather than extends existing ones. However, the CD index has known problems: it suffers from citation inflation, is confounded by citing "star journals" (Science, Nature), and conflates disruption with interdisciplinarity. The vision's type-system approach would be a *richer* encoding of the same intuition — not just "disruptive or not" but "specifically, what did it replace and what did it keep?"

**Steal-worthy idea.** The CD index's backward-looking, citation-structure-based approach to measuring innovation character is conceptually aligned with Stage 3. But the type system should produce a *multidimensional* disruption signal rather than a scalar.

### Uzzi et al. (2013) — Atypical Combinations

**What it is.** Analysis of 17.9M papers across 50 years of Web of Science. Key finding: the highest-impact papers are grounded in conventional combinations of prior work but feature an "intrusion" of unusual journal-pair combinations (measured via z-scores of journal co-citation frequency). Papers with this pattern — mostly conventional, partly atypical — were 2x more likely to become highly cited.

**Relevance.** This directly validates the vision's recombination-centric framing. The vision represents innovations as ASTs of constructor calls — *compositions* of existing methods. Uzzi's finding suggests that the most impactful compositions are those that combine mostly-familiar components with one or two unusual ones. The type system could compute an "atypicality score" for any composed expression by measuring how often its component classes co-appear in existing papers. This would be a concrete, quantitative output of Stage 3.

**Limitation.** Uzzi's method operates at the journal level (pairwise journal co-citation), which is extremely coarse. The vision's method-level representation would enable a much more fine-grained version of the same analysis.

### The Recombination Framework (Schumpeter → Modern)

**What it is.** A large literature, originating with Schumpeter (1912), views innovation as "novel combination of existing resources." Modern empirical work (e.g., knowledge recombination studies using patent IPC co-occurrence networks) finds that intermediate-distance recombination has the highest impact — not too local, not too distant. There's a U-shaped relationship between knowledge-spanning distance and disruptive innovation, moderated by team size.

**Relevance.** This literature provides the theoretical grounding for the vision's composition-based representation. If innovation *is* recombination, then representing innovations as composed expressions of typed components is the natural formal structure. The "knowledge distance" findings suggest that the type system's topology (which classes are "near" vs. "far") encodes information about likely innovation trajectories.

### The Metascience Novelty Indicators Challenge

**What it is.** An ongoing global initiative to develop and validate scalable indicators of research novelty. Approaches include examining new pairs in cited-document/journal/field combinations, semantic content analysis via NLP and AI, and hybrid metrics combining structural and content-based signals.

**Relevance.** This is the closest active research program to the vision's goals. The challenge is trying to build *exactly* the kind of novelty/innovation measurement the vision describes, but via different methods (mostly bibliometric signals rather than structured method representations). The vision's approach is complementary — a type-system representation would enable novelty indicators that existing bibliometric methods can't produce (e.g., "this paper introduces a new class in a previously empty region of the composition space").

## 3. Ontology Learning / Ontology Evolution

### LLMs4OL Challenge (2024-2025)

**What it is.** A shared task at ISWC evaluating LLMs for ontology learning across four tasks: Text2Onto, Term Typing, Taxonomy Discovery, and Non-Taxonomic Relation Extraction. Top systems (Claude 3 Sonnet, Mixtral-8×7B) achieve F1 scores of 0.85-0.97 on semantic relation identification.

**Relevance.** This is direct evidence on whether LLMs can reliably extract the kind of structured type information the vision needs. The high F1 scores on relation identification are encouraging for Stage 1 extraction. But the LLMs4OL tasks are simpler than what the vision requires — they classify relations between *existing* ontology terms, whereas Stage 1 must also *create* new terms (new classes in the type system) and determine their parameter signatures. Term typing (classifying a term into an ontology) is a subtask of what the vision needs, but not the whole thing.

### AutOnto and LLM-Based Ontology Engineering

**What it is.** Recent systems like AutOnto integrate BERT-based topic modeling with LLMs to extract concepts and relationships from text corpora. OntoKGen uses iterative Chain-of-Thought prompting to build ontologies interactively. OntoGenix automates ontology development pipelines.

**Relevance.** These systems demonstrate that LLM-driven ontology construction is feasible. However, they build *taxonomic* ontologies (is-a hierarchies), not *compositional* type systems (parameterized constructors). The vision needs both: it needs taxonomy discovery (recognizing that DPO and RLHF occupy the same role) AND compositional structure (DPO's constructor signature). Current ontology learning focuses almost exclusively on the former.

### Concept Drift and Ontology Evolution

**What it is.** SemaDrift and related tools measure semantic drift between ontology versions using embedding-based and morphing-chain approaches. Knowledge graph embedding methods handle concept drift in streaming data. Wikidata provides real-time edit histories; DBpedia offers periodic snapshots.

**Relevance.** The vision's dual representation (A = descriptive, versioned; B = compressed) is essentially an ontology evolution problem. The concept drift literature provides tools for measuring *how much* the type system changes between versions, which is relevant to the convergence analysis (does the type system stabilize?). The embedding-based approaches for detecting semantic shift could complement the formal type-system representation — detecting when two class names refer to things that are semantically drifting apart or converging.

**Key gap.** Existing ontology evolution work tracks changes in class/property definitions. The vision needs to track changes in *compositional patterns* — not just "did the class definition change?" but "did the way this class gets composed with others change?" This is a harder problem that the existing literature doesn't address directly.

## 5. Things You Haven't Thought to Ask About

### Ologs (Ontology Logs) — Categorical Framework for Knowledge Representation

**What it is.** Spivak & Kent (2012) introduced ologs as a category-theoretic framework for knowledge representation. An olog is a category where objects are types (with English-language labels) and morphisms are functional relationships. They're rigorously formalized, composable, and cross-comparable — unlike informal semantic networks.

**Why this matters.** The vision's "composable constructors" representation is *close to* a categorical framework but doesn't use the language or tools of category theory. Ologs provide a mathematical foundation for exactly the kind of typed, compositional knowledge representation the vision describes. The Curry-Howard-Lambek isomorphism (type theory ↔ category theory ↔ logic) means the vision's "type system" framing and a categorical framing are formally equivalent — but category theory provides composition operators (functors, natural transformations) that might be useful for the compression step (Representation B). At minimum, the vision should acknowledge this connection and consider whether categorical tools would help formalize the "composition" semantics.

### CS-KG 2.0 — Large-Scale Computer Science Knowledge Graph

**What it is.** A recent (2025) large-scale knowledge graph specifically for computer science, built on DBLP data with entity and relation extraction from paper texts.

**Relevance.** Provides a corpus-level knowledge graph that the vision could use as a data source for initializing the type system, rather than processing papers from scratch.

### Karpathy's Autoresearch (March 2026)

**What it is.** A 630-line script that lets an AI agent autonomously run experiments: modify code, train for 5 minutes, evaluate, keep or discard improvements. Ran 700 experiments in 2 days; Shopify achieved 19% quality improvement on an 0.8B parameter model overnight.

**Why this matters.** The vision explicitly cites "Karpathy's autoresearch loop" as inspiration for Stage 4 (Representation B compression). The actual autoresearch system is now public and well-documented. The key architectural insight from autoresearch — a tight evaluate-modify-commit loop with automatic versioning — maps directly to the compression agents in Stage 4. However, autoresearch operates on *code that runs* (training scripts with measurable loss), whereas the vision's compression agents operate on *type definitions that represent*. The objective function for compression is harder to define — "parsimony weighted by explanatory power" is conceptually clear but operationally vague. Autoresearch works because loss is a scalar; the vision needs to define its equivalent.

### Connected Papers / ResearchRabbit / Litmaps

**What they are.** Tools that create visual citation-graph maps of related papers, helping researchers explore the neighborhood around a seed paper.

**Relevance.** These tools solve the "find related work" problem that Stage 1 needs for chronological paper ingestion. Connected Papers' similarity algorithm (not just direct citations, but co-citation and bibliographic coupling) could help identify which papers belong in an evolutionary chain without manual curation. For the first experiment (30-50 RLHF→DPO papers), these tools could automate the paper selection step.

### NLP-AKG — Few-Shot Academic Knowledge Graph for NLP

**What it is.** Extracts 620K entities and 2.3M relations from 60K ACL Anthology papers, including Field, Keywords, Problem, Method, Model, Task, Dataset, Metric, and Result entities.

**Relevance.** This is the closest existing extraction system to what Stage 1 needs, applied to a comparable corpus (NLP papers). The entity types — especially Method, Model, and their relationships — are directly relevant. However, NLP-AKG extracts flat entity-relation triples, not parameterized constructor specifications. The gap between "extracts Method entities" and "extracts constructor signatures with typed parameters" is the core novelty of the vision.

### Benchmark Saturation Literature (2025-2026)

**What it is.** Growing research on how benchmarks lose discriminative power as models approach ceiling scores, and how data contamination undermines benchmark validity.

**Relevance.** The vision's chain-linking approach for evals is partly motivated by this problem, but the saturation literature provides specific quantitative methods for detecting when a benchmark has saturated. These should inform the chain-linking decision of *when* to splice in new benchmarks. A benchmark that's saturated isn't contributing useful signal to the eval matrix.
